03 · CHESS
The Game Beneath The Game

A life in 64 squares.

Most people play chess. I study it. Openings, middlegame structure, endgame technique — through Gotham Chess, Chess.com courses, and thousands of analyzed positions. The board is the closest thing I have to a mirror.

I have played chess competitively for over a decade. Tournament wins. Top finishes. The kind of discipline that comes only from sitting at a board for hours, calculating, failing, recalibrating, and trying again.

But chess as a competition is downstream of chess as a way of thinking. What I have actually internalized through the game is a philosophy of patience — the willingness to forgo immediate gratification for positional advantage. The discipline to let pieces develop before launching attacks. The humility to study the masters before assuming you have anything new to offer.

Every business decision I'll make, every relationship I navigate, every strategic move in life — they all rhyme with positions I have seen on the board. Chess does not teach you to win. It teaches you to see the whole board, weigh trade-offs honestly, and accept that the best move is rarely the most exciting one.

It is the single most useful intellectual discipline I have ever practiced — and I'll practice it for the rest of my life.

i.
Play for position, not points.

The strongest moves often look quiet. Real advantage compounds invisibly until it becomes irreversible.

ii.
Develop before you attack.

Premature aggression loses to patient preparation. Build your foundation first; the opportunities will reveal themselves.

iii.
Every move costs something.

There are no free moves. Every choice creates new constraints — the discipline is in choosing well.

iv.
Endgame thinking from move one.

The opening matters because it shapes the middlegame, which shapes the endgame. Always be playing the position you're building toward.

Football — the lifelong sport.

Football has been a constant since I was a child. I have played at every level available to me and have been outstanding at each one. It is where I learned how teams function under pressure, how leadership is earned through performance, and how to compete without losing composure.

The lessons from team sport — communication, accountability, performance under fatigue — carry into every other area of my life.

The gym — proof of system.

Anyone can train for a week. Almost no one trains consistently for a year. The gym is the most honest mirror I have — it tells you exactly how disciplined you have been, with no possibility of self-deception.

I bring that same standard to everything else. If I cannot trust myself with the body, I cannot trust myself with anything more abstract.
